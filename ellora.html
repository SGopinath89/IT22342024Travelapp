Facts

* Sinharaja Forest Reserve is a national forest and a UNESCO World Heritage Site in Sri Lanka. It is also a biodiversity hotspot, considered the last viable area of primary tropical rainforest in the country.

* The forest is estimated to be 200 million years old, dating back to the Jurassic period. It's incredibly dense, consisting of over 60% endemic plant species, meaning they are found nowhere else on Earth. The forest canopy is so thick that only 5-15% of sunlight reaches the ground!

* Over 50% of Sri Lanka's endemic species of fauna call Sinharaja home. This includes mammals, butterflies, amphibians, reptiles, and especially birds, with over 95% of Sri Lanka's endemic bird species found here. Keep an eye out for Sri Lanka Birdwing Butterflies, Purple-faced Leaf Monkeys, and Sri Lankan Leopard during your visit!

* Sinharaja receives a whopping 2500 to 6000 mm of rainfall annually, distributed throughout the year. This creates a lush and fertile environment perfect for the rainforest's rich biodiversity. The average temperature is between 18-27 degrees Celsius with a humidity of 75% - 90%.

* Sadly, Sinharaja faces threats from habitat loss due to human encroachment and invasive species. Conservation efforts are underway to protect this irreplaceable ecosystem.

Myths

The Lion King: The name "Sinharaja" itself translates to "Lion King." Legend speaks of a magnificent lion that once ruled the forest. Some stories say it terrorized nearby villages, while others depict it as a noble creature. This lion is even said to be the ancestor of the Sinhala people, the island nation's main ethnic group.

The Domain of Kings: Another set of legends suggests that the Sinharaja belonged to Sri Lanka's ancient kings. The name "Rajasinghe Forest" found in colonial records strengthens this belief, "Rajasinghe" referring to a past Sri Lankan monarch.

Echoes of the Past: Perhaps due to the dense foliage and the many sounds of the forest, some myths talk about Sinharaja being haunted. Whispers and echoes are said to be the voices of those long gone.
