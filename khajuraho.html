Facts

**History:** The Royal Botanic Gardens have a long and interesting history. The land was first used as a royal pleasure garden in the 14th century by King Wickramabahu III. In the 18th century, it became a royal residence for King Kirti Sri Rajasinha and King Rajadhi Rajasinghe. The gardens were established in their current form in 1821 by Alexander Moon, a Scottish medical officer who was superintendent of the Ceylon cinnamon gardens.

**Location and Size:** The Royal Botanic Gardens are located about 5.5 kilometers west of the city of Kandy, Sri Lanka. They encompass a total area of 147 acres (0.59 square kilometers). The gardens are situated at an elevation of 460 meters above sea level and receive around 200 days of annual rainfall.

**Flora:** The Royal Botanic Gardens are home to an impressive collection of over 4,000 plant species, including orchids, spices, medicinal plants, and palm trees. The gardens are especially renowned for their orchid collection, which is one of the largest in Asia.

**National Herbarium:** The National Herbarium of Sri Lanka is located within the Royal Botanic Gardens. The herbarium houses a collection of over 100,000 plant specimens, which is an invaluable resource for botanists and researchers.

**Visitors:** The Royal Botanic Gardens are a popular tourist destination, attracting over 1.6 million visitors per year. The gardens are a great place to learn about Sri Lanka's unique flora and to enjoy a peaceful walk amidst the lush greenery.

Myths

Giant Cannibalistic Spider: A less-than-pleasant tale mentions a gigantic spider said to reside deep within the gardens. This myth likely arose from the presence of large Golden Orb-Web spiders, which are native to Sri Lanka. While impressive in size, these spiders are not venomous to humans and pose no threat.

Hidden Royal Treasure: This whispers of a hidden treasure trove buried somewhere within the gardens by Sri Lankan royalty. While the gardens were once a royal pleasure ground, there's no historical evidence to support this myth.
